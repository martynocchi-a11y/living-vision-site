Aaron Velky

Founder

Aaron Velky isn't coaching from the other side of the struggle.

He's in it.

For the last several years he's been doing what the men he serves are doing — building a business, showing up as a father, navigating the tension between the two without a roadmap. He knows what it costs to defer the uncomfortable. He knows what the multiplied bill looks like when it arrives. And he knows what it takes to stay in the fight for what matters when the easiest thing in the world would be to go quiet.

That's not a backstory. That's the credential.

Aaron has worked with hundreds of founders, CEOs, and business owners — men who've built something real and found themselves at a threshold they didn't expect. His work isn't about strategy in the conventional sense. It's about what happens when you stop performing certainty you don't have and start telling the truth about where you actually are.

He runs intensive retreats — never more than ten men, three days — where the armor comes off and the real work begins. He coaches leaders one on one through the kind of change that doesn't show up on an org chart. And everything he does is built on one belief:

The drift can be named. And naming it is where the work begins.

Aaron lives in Scottsdale, Arizona with his son Emerson — who, at three and a half, is already teaching him more than any framework ever has.

Marty Nocchi

Integration & Culture Architect

Marty Nocchi spent fifteen years as a Catholic priest and twenty-two in ministry before he did the thing this work is built around — he climbed to the top of his mountain, found the hollow, and chose to go looking for what actually came next.

That experience — of building something meaningful, giving everything to it, and then standing in the silence on the other side asking what it was all for — is what he brings to every client engagement. Not as theory. As lived territory.

He met Aaron Velky at that crossroads. Two men at similar thresholds, asking similar questions. What began as a shared search became a four-year partnership — in the retreat room, in the business, with clients — built on a specific and rare quality of knowing.

As Integration & Culture Architect at Living Vision, Marty's role is to see what the man on the field can't see because he's standing in it. The drift. The gap between the performed version and the true one. The implicit experience that hasn't yet found language. His job is to name it precisely enough that the person recognizes themselves in it — and to build the architecture that lets the real version show up consistently.

He continues to write on Substack in his own voice — exploring the questions that don't resolve neatly, the kind of thinking that formed over decades of sitting with people in their most honest moments. That background — twenty-two years of being present to people at their most unguarded — is what makes the strategic work trustworthy rather than merely clever.

He doesn't coach from a framework. He works from knowing. And knowing, in his experience, always begins with the willingness to name what's actually true.
